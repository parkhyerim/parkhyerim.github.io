XR UX Case Study

Awareness in VR

Designing subtle awareness cues that help VR users notice nearby people without breaking immersion.

This project explores how social awareness can be introduced into VR through interaction design, balancing visibility, timing, and immersion.

Project overview

Role
Interaction design, prototyping, user study
Context
LMU Munich / immersive interaction research
Timeline
2023
Output
Prototype, controlled study, design guidelines

Challenge

VR users can become disconnected from nearby people. Subtle real-world cues are lost, but still needed.

Contribution

I designed and evaluated three awareness concepts balancing visibility, spatial clarity, and immersion.

Research contribution

The study shows that a mixed visualization best balances awareness, locating, and immersion.

Problem

How do you notice someone nearby without breaking immersion?

VR creates immersion by reducing awareness of the physical world. This becomes a UX problem when another person nearby wants to interact.

Instead of distance, orientation becomes the key signal: people turn toward you before interacting.

The challenge: communicate intent early, without disrupting the experience.

Process

From social cue to interaction design

Reframed the problem

Shifted from distance to orientation as the primary signal of intent.

Explored concepts

Designed three approaches balancing immediacy and spatial clarity.

Prototyped

Implemented concepts in a VR gameplay scenario.

Evaluated

Measured awareness, locating, and immersion.

Concepts

Three approaches

Animoji

Fast awareness through in-view cues.

Avatar

Spatial awareness through location cues.

Mixed

Combines both for best balance.

Validation

What worked best

Fast detection

In-view cues improved reaction time.

Better locating

Spatial cues improved understanding of position.

Outcomes

Key insight

Mixed interaction works best

Balances speed and clarity.

Awareness should be progressive

Gradual cues outperform sudden alerts.

Reflection

What I learned

Awareness is not just visibility—it is timing and progression. The best systems guide users gradually rather than interrupting them.
